\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:Introduction}

Interest in the quantitative assessment of financial risk has a long history, with key foundations emerging in the early $20^{\text{th}}$ century. An early milestone was Louis Bachelier’s dissertation, which introduced probabilistic modelling into finance and laid the groundwork for modern mathematical finance. More broadly, risk management aims to identify, measure, and evaluate potential losses in order to support decision-making under uncertainty. To this end, risk managers employ specific risk measures to assess the exposure associated with a particular decision or investment.\\
One of the most widely used measures of market risk, defined as the risk of losses arising from on- and off-balance-sheet positions, is value-at-risk (VaR). Early contributions to the measurement of downside risk can be found in \citet{Leavens1945}, while related concepts appear in the safety-first criterion of \citet{Roy1952} and the mean--variance framework of \citet{Markowitz1952}. However, these works did not define VaR in its modern form. VaR emerged as an operational tool for market risk measurement in the early 1990s, when J.P. Morgan introduced it internally as a standardized approach to measuring firm-wide market risk. The public release of the \textit{RiskMetrics} methodology in 1994 contributed to the widespread adoption of VaR by financial institutions. VaR was subsequently incorporated into financial regulation through the 1996 Market Risk Amendment to the Basel I Accord, which allowed banks to use internal VaR models for the calculation of regulatory capital. Since the forecasting of VaR requires an assessment of the conditional distribution of returns, models for time-varying volatility play a central role in its practical implementation.\\
In practical applications, one common approach to forecasting VaR is to model the conditional volatility of financial returns using autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity models. The GARCH model, introduced by \citet{Bollerslev1986} as a generalization of the ARCH model of \citet{Engle1982}, differs fundamentally from standard time series models such as ARMA or VAR models. Rather than focusing on the conditional mean, ARCH and GARCH models describe the conditional variance by relating current volatility to past squared returns and past volatility. This approach is motivated by well-known stylized facts of financial time series: while returns typically display little autocorrelation, squared or absolute returns often exhibit strong and persistent dependence, and volatility tends to cluster over time \citep{mandelbrot1963variation,taylor2011asset}. By explicitly modeling conditional volatility, GARCH-type models provide a natural framework for time-varying financial risk and thus for the forecasting of VaR. However, the increasing availability of high-frequency financial data has created new settings in which risk must be assessed at an intraday level, thereby calling for frameworks that can make fuller use of such data.\\
The increasing availability of high-frequency financial data, together with the growth of high-frequency trading and the demand for timely risk assessment, has intensified the need for methods that capture intraday volatility dynamics. When intraday observations are viewed as realizations of curves over a trading day, Functional Data Analysis (FDA) offers a natural framework for their analysis. FDA studies data that are represented as functions or curves; see, for example, \citet{Ramsay2005}, \citet{hsing2015theoretical}, and \citet{kokoszka2017introduction}.\\
Against this background, recent work has combined ideas from volatility modelling and functional time series analysis. \citet{hormann2013functional} extend the ARCH framework to the functional setting, and \citet{Aue2017} subsequently develop a functional version of the GARCH model. This framework is particularly relevant for high-frequency financial applications, where intraday return dynamics can be represented as curves. In a related application, \citet{RICE20201023} apply the Functional GARCH (FGARCH) model to the forecasting of intraday value-at-risk curves.\\
Despite these developments, the available results are technically demanding and often presuppose substantial background knowledge in functional data analysis and financial econometrics. As a result, the model is not easily accessible to readers seeking a structured introduction to its theoretical background, estimation, and practical use. The present thesis therefore aims to summarize and contextualize the existing literature while also examining aspects that are particularly relevant for applications. In particular, it discusses the relationship between the FGARCH model and closely related alternatives such as the PC-GARCH model, studies finite-sample behavior under heavy-tailed data-generating processes in a Monte Carlo setting, and highlights practical implementation issues. In this sense, the thesis is intended both as an introduction to the FGARCH framework and as an investigation of its use for intraday value-at-risk forecasting.\\
At the same time, the scope of the thesis is deliberately limited. It does not aim to develop new theoretical results for the FGARCH model, nor does it attempt to provide a comprehensive comparison with all competing approaches to multivariate or functional volatility modelling. Instead, the focus lies on presenting and contextualizing the existing theory, comparing the FGARCH and PC-GARCH frameworks, and studying their finite-sample behavior in a Monte Carlo setting with a view toward intraday value-at-risk forecasting. Likewise, although the limitations of value-at-risk are discussed, the thesis does not seek to provide a general assessment of alternative risk measures.\\
Against this background, the aim of this thesis is to examine the theoretical properties and finite-sample performance of the FGARCH model, with a particular focus on forecasting intraday value-at-risk. In particular, this thesis contributes to the existing literature by providing an overview of the theoretical results available for FGARCH models, highlighting selected open problems, and studying their use for intraday value-at-risk forecasting in a Monte Carlo setting. Furthermore, the FGARCH model is related to the principal component GARCH (PC-GARCH) model, since both approaches rely on principal components to reduce dimensionality. Their relationship is examined from a theoretical perspective, and their performance is compared by means of Monte Carlo simulations. Finally, practical issues that arise when applying FGARCH models are discussed, and possible ways to address them are outlined.\\
These objectives can be expressed in terms of the following research questions: What are the main theoretical properties of the FGARCH model, and which difficulties arise when moving from multivariate to functional data? Which estimation strategies for FGARCH models exist, and what are their respective advantages and disadvantages? What practical challenges arise when applying FGARCH models to forecast intraday value-at-risk? How does the FGARCH model perform in finite samples, especially under heavy-tailed data-generating processes? How does its performance compare with that of the PC-GARCH model? What possible extensions of the FGARCH model remain open for future research?\\

\section{Functional GARCH}
\label{sec:FGARCH}
\subsection{(Scalar) GARCH and Multivariate GARCH}
\label{subsec:ScalarGARCH}
Before turning to the FGARCH model, I first consider the scalar version of the GARCH model in both the univariate and multivariate setting.\\
An univariate GARCH$(p,q)$ model can be described by its first two moments:
\begin{definition}[GARCH(p,q) Process]
    A process $(y_t)$ is called a GARCH(p,q) process if its first two conditional moments exist and satisfy:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\mathrm{E}(y_t \,|\, y_u,u<t)=0, \quad \forall t\in\mathbb{Z}$.
        \item There exist constants $\delta,\alpha_i,i=1,...,q$ and $\beta_j,j=1,...,p$ such that\\
        \begin{align}
            \sigma_t^2=\mathrm{Var}(y_t\,|\,y_u,u<t)=\delta+\sum_{i=0}^q\alpha_iy_{t-i}^2+\sum_{j=1}^p\beta_j\sigma_{t-j}^2
        \end{align}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}
To relate the GARCH$(p,q)$ model to more familiar linear time series structures, define the innovation term $\nu_t=y_t^2-\sigma_t^2$ and substitute $\sigma_t^2=y_t^2-\nu_t$ into the volatility equation
\begin{align}
\label{ARMAstructure}
    y_t^2=\delta+\sum_{i=0}^r\overbrace{(\alpha_i+\beta_i)}^{\text{$\kappa_i$}}y_{t-i}^2+\nu_t-\sum_{j=1}^p\beta_j\nu_{t-j}=\delta+\sum_{i=0}^r\kappa_iy_{t-i}^2+\nu_t-\sum_{j=1}^p\beta_j\nu_{t-j}
\end{align}
where $r=\mathrm{max}(p,q)$ with $\alpha_i=0$ ($\beta_j=0$) if $i>q$ ($j>p$). \labelcref{ARMAstructure} shows that the squared process $(y_t^2)$ admits an ARMA$(r,p)$-type representation. This also helps explain why the GARCH model is viewed as a generalization of the ARCH model: just as an ARMA process extends an AR process, the GARCH model extends the ARCH model. Moreover, similarly to the relationship between AR and ARMA models, a GARCH$(p,q)$ process can be represented as an ARCH$(\infty)$ process \citep{francq2019garch}. It should be noted, however, that $(\nu_t)$ is generally not i.i.d. Consequently, the representation in \labelcref{ARMAstructure} cannot be treated as a standard ARMA model when examining the existence of stationary solutions to the GARCH$(p,q)$ process.\\
A more restrictive, yet often useful, version of the GARCH model is the strong GARCH$(p,q)$ process. A strong univariate GARCH$(p,q)$ process is given by:
\begin{gather}
    y_t = \sigma_t\varepsilon_t \\
    \sigma_t^2 = \delta + \sum_{i=1}^q \alpha_i y_{t-i}^2 + \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j \sigma_{t-j}^2
    \label{Eq2StrongGARCH}
\end{gather}
where $(\varepsilon_t)$ is $\mathrm{i.i.d.}$ In addition, it is often assumed that $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t|\varepsilon_j, j<t]=0$ and $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_t]=1$.\\
In the following, the focus will be on the strong GARCH$(1,1)$ process, since the corresponding functional version is studied in \citet{Aue2017} and applied in \citet{RICE20201023}.
The strong GARCH$(1,1)$ process is the simplest specification within the class of strong GARCH models. Despite its simplicity, it is also one of the most widely used specifications in practice \citep{Zivot2009}.\\
To study the existence of a stationary solution to the strong GARCH$(1,1)$ process, it is useful to rewrite the volatility equation as
\begin{align}
    \sigma_t^2 = \delta + \gamma_{t-1} \sigma_{t-1}^2, \quad \text{where }\gamma_t=\alpha\varepsilon_t^2+\beta.
\end{align}
This representation shows that the volatility equation of the strong GARCH$(1,1)$ process can be viewed as an autoregressive model with random coefficients. This representation is useful for studying the existence of a strictly stationary and ergodic solution.\\
The following condition guarantees the existence of a strictly stationary, non-anticipative, and ergodic solution to the strong GARCH$(1,1)$ process:
\begin{theorem}[Strict stationarity of the strong GARCH(1,1) process]
\label{stationarityscalarGARCH11}
    If
    \begin{gather}
        -\infty\leq\mathbb{E}[ \log\{\overbrace{\alpha\varepsilon_t^2+\beta}^{\gamma_t}\}]<0,
    \end{gather}
    then the infinite sum
    \begin{gather}
        h_t = \delta \Bigl(1 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty}\Gamma_{t,i}\Bigr), \quad \text{where }\Gamma_{t,i}=\gamma_{t-1}\cdots\gamma_{t-i}
    \end{gather}
    converges almost surely, and the process $(y_t)$ defined by $y_t=\sqrt{h_t}\varepsilon_t$ is the unique strictly stationary solution of the strong GARCH(1,1) model. This solution is non-anticipative and ergodic.
\end{theorem}
To prove \Cref{stationarityscalarGARCH11}, note that \labelcref{Eq2StrongGARCH} can be solved recursively to obtain $h_t$. Showing that $h_t$ converges involves an application of the Cauchy root test as well as the strong law of large numbers. Uniqueness is then established by contradiction: assuming the existence of another solution, one shows that it must coincide almost surely with the original one.\\
Ergodicity describes the property of a stochastic process that time averages along a single, sufficiently long realization converge to the same values as ensemble averages taken over many realizations. A particularly strong sufficient condition for ergodicity is geometric ergodicity, which requires not only convergence to a unique stationary distribution, but convergence at an exponential rate. It can be shown that $(\sigma_t;t\in \mathbb{Z})$ is geometrically ergodic under suitable regularity conditions. Intuitively, this means that the effect of initial conditions vanishes exponentially fast and that sample averages converge to their population counterparts. Moreover, $(y_t;t\in\mathbb{Z})$ is geometrically $\beta$-mixing, a strong dependence property that implies ergodicity. To prove that the GARCH(1,1) process is geometrically ergodic first note that the volatility equation of the GARCH process can be rewritten into a Markov Chain representation. One can then make use of a Theorem from \citet{FeignTweedie1985}. They show that a Markov chain that is also an aperiodic, irreducible Feller chain is geometrically ergodic if it satisfies a drift condition. Proving that $(y_t;t\in\mathbb{Z})$ is geometrically $\beta$-mixing follows from the geometric ergodicity of $(\sigma_t;t\in\mathbb{Z})$ and the fact that $(\varepsilon_t;t\in\mathbb{Z})$ is i.i.d. $(y_t;t\in\mathbb{Z})$ is not geometrically ergodic since it is not Markovian. The complete proof can be found in \citet{francq2019garch}. A precise mathematical definition of ergodicity is given in the appendix.

\begin{remark}{Physical intuition behind mixing and ergodicity}{rem:IntuitionMixingErgodicity}
    A useful intuition for statistical mixing and ergodicity can be obtained from the physical act of stirring different liquids in a glass. Imagine that the glass is divided into many tiny regions and that we observe which liquid occupies each region after every stir. If we record the configuration after each stir, the resulting sequence of observations can be interpreted as a realization of a stochastic process, while each stir corresponds to one iteration of its transition mechanism.

    Ergodicity intuitively means that, after many stirring steps, the liquids become distributed throughout the glass according to their overall proportions. In other words, the long-run fraction of time that a given liquid is observed in a particular region corresponds to the amount of that liquid in the glass and the size of the region. Geometric ergodicity strengthens this idea by stating that the distribution of liquids across the glass approaches this long-run configuration at an exponential rate.

    The notion of mixing describes how quickly the influence of the past on the future disappears. Mixing coefficients such as the $\alpha$- and $\beta$-mixing coefficients quantify how close observations that are far apart in time are to being statistically independent. In the stirring analogy, after sufficiently many stirs the spatial arrangement of liquids becomes nearly independent of how the liquids were initially poured into the glass. Roughly speaking, the $\alpha$-mixing coefficient measures the remaining dependence between past and future events, while the $\beta$-mixing coefficient provides a stronger measure of how far their joint behavior is from statistical independence. Both therefore quantify how effectively the system "forgets" its earlier states.

    This intuition also illustrates the relationship between ergodicity and mixing. If the liquids mix very efficiently—like highly fluid liquids—then the distribution in the glass quickly approaches its long-run configuration and the dependence between distant observations vanishes rapidly. This corresponds to geometric ergodicity and typically implies strong mixing properties. By contrast, if the liquids are more viscous, the system may still eventually explore all regions of the glass (ergodicity), but the approach to the long-run distribution and the loss of dependence can occur much more slowly.
\end{remark}

Next, I will provide a short exposition of multivariate GARCH processes, as they are very closely related to FGARCH processes in practice. Defining a multivariate GARCH process is not as straightforward as in the univariate case. This is because moving from the univariate to the multivariate setting requires not only the modeling of additional conditional variances, but also the conditional covariances between the components. Without further restrictions, the resulting number of parameters quickly becomes too large for reliable estimation and asymptotic analysis. Therefore, the conditional covariance matrix $H_t$ follows an explicit specification. In the following, I will present two of these specifications since they are closely connected to the FGARCH model. Throughout this discussion, I will assume that $y_t,\varepsilon_t\in \mathbb{R}^m$ and that $\varepsilon_t$ is an i.i.d. vector.\\
First, I consider the PC-GARCH model, which is defined by
\begin{gather}
\label{PCGARCHyequation}
    y_t = H_t^{\frac{1}{2}}\varepsilon_t,\\
    \label{PCGARCHHequation}
    H_t = P \Lambda_t P^{\top}.
\end{gather}
Here, $P$ is an $m\times m$ orthogonal matrix, while $\Lambda_t = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_{1,t},\lambda_{2,t},\ldots,\lambda_{m,t})$ is an $m\times m$ diagonal matrix. The matrix $P$ is typically estimated using Principal Component Analysis, and the diagonal elements of $\Lambda_t$ are assumed to follow univariate GARCH(1,1) processes:
\begin{gather}
\label{PCGARCHlambdaequation}
    \lambda_{i,t} = \delta_i + \alpha_i f_{i,t-1}^{2} + \beta_i \lambda_{i,t-1}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, m.
\end{gather}
In this specification, $f_{i,t}$ denotes the $i$-th component of the transformed series $f_t = P^\top y_t$. The parameters in \Cref{PCGARCHlambdaequation} are typically estimated by quasi-maximum likelihood. Alternative specifications for $\lambda_{i,t}$ are also possible.\\
From the representation in \labelcref{PCGARCHHequation}, it follows directly that the PC-GARCH model decomposes the conditional covariance matrix $H_t$ into a set of constant eigenvectors and time-varying eigenvalues. As will be discussed in \Cref{sec:Simulation}, the PC-GARCH model is closely related to a discretized version of the FGARCH model, in which the underlying functions are observed on a finite grid.\\
The second multivariate GARCH specification considered is the Constant Conditional Correlation (CCC) GARCH model, which is defined as
\begin{gather}
    y_t = H_t^{\frac{1}{2}}\varepsilon_t,\\
    \label{CCCGARCHHequation}
    H_t = D_t R D_t^{\top}.
\end{gather}
Here, $D_t=\text{diag}(\sqrt{h_{1,t}}, \sqrt{h_{2,t}}, \ldots, \sqrt{h_{m,t}})$ is an $m \times m$ diagonal matrix and $R$ is an $m \times m$ constant correlation matrix. The vector of conditional variances $h_t$ is assumed to follow a GARCH(1,1)-type specification given by
\begin{gather}
\label{CCCGARCHsmallhequation}
    h_t = \delta + A (y_{t-1} \odot y_{t-1}) + B h_{t-1},
\end{gather}
where $\odot$ denotes the elementwise (Hadamard) product, $\delta$ is an $m$-dimensional vector, and $A$ and $B$ are $m \times m$ parameter matrices. \labelcref{CCCGARCHHequation} shows that $H_t$ consists of time-varying, variable-specific conditional variances combined with a constant correlation structure. In addition, \Cref{CCCGARCHsmallhequation} shows that the variable-specific conditional variances evolve dynamically, depending not only on each variable’s own past but also on the past of the other variables. As will be discussed in \Cref{subsec:FGARCHEstimation}, once the functional parameters are projected onto a finite set of basis functions, estimation of the FGARCH model reduces to that of a semi-strong CCC-GARCH model with $R = \mathbb{I}$, where $\mathbb{I}$ denotes the identity matrix.\\
Since in practice only the process $(y_t)_{t\in\mathbb{Z}}$ is observed, the parameters of any univariate or multivariate GARCH model have to be estimated. GARCH models are typically estimated using (quasi-)maximum likelihood estimation. For GARCH models, (quasi-)maximum likelihood estimation is generally preferred to least-squares estimation, since least-squares methods require stronger moment conditions and are typically less efficient \citep{francq2019garch}. In the following, I will illustrate the estimation procedure for the univariate GARCH(1,1) model. Analogous arguments apply to other GARCH specifications.\\
The Gaussian quasi-likelihood for the GARCH$(1,1)$ model is given by
\begin{gather}
    L_n(\underline{\theta};y_1,..,y_n)=\prod_{t=1}^n\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \tilde{\sigma}_t^2}}\exp\left(-\frac{y_t^2}{2\tilde{\sigma}_t^2}\right); \quad \tilde{\sigma}_t^2=\tilde{\sigma}_t^2(\underline{\theta})=\underline{\delta}+\underline{\alpha} y_{t-1}^2+\underline{\beta}\tilde{\sigma}_{t-1}^2.
\end{gather}
Here, $\underline{\theta}=(\underline{\delta},\underline{\alpha},\underline{\beta})^\top$ denotes the parameter vector, and $\tilde{\sigma}_t^2$ is computed recursively. The true parameter vector is denoted by $\theta=(\delta,\alpha,\beta)^\top$.\\
Taking logarithms and omitting constants, the optimization problem is equivalent to minimizing
\begin{gather}
    \mathcal{I}_n=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n\left( \frac{y_t^2}{\tilde{\sigma}_t^2}+\log \tilde{\sigma}_t^2\right).
\end{gather}
The parameter estimator is then obtained numerically by minimizing this criterion, for example by using a Newton-Raphson algorithm.\\
Under suitable regularity conditions, the quasi-maximum likelihood estimator is consistent and asymptotically normal. If the innovations are Gaussian, its asymptotic variance is given by the inverse Fisher information matrix. If the innovations are non-Gaussian, the asymptotic variance instead involves the inverse of an adjusted information matrix. The details of the proof are omitted here for the sake of conciseness.
\subsection{Model}
\label{subsec:FGARCHModel}
This section introduces the FGARCH model and discusses its key theoretical properties. I begin with the definition of the model.\\
A FGARCH process is defined as follows:
\begin{definition}[FGARCH(p,q) Process]
\label{fGARCHpq}
A sequence of random functions $(y_i(t))_{i\in \mathbb{Z}}$ is called a FGARCH process of order $(p,q)$ if it satisfies the following equations
    \begin{gather}
        y_i(t) = \sigma_i(t)\varepsilon_i(t)\\
        \sigma_i^2(t) = \delta(t)+\sum_{j=1}^q\alpha_jy_{i-j}^2(t)+\sum_{k=1}^p\beta_k\sigma_{i-k}^2(t).
    \end{gather}
\end{definition}
Here, $\delta(t)\in L^2[0,1]$ is a non-negative function, and $(\alpha_j)_{j\in (1,...,p)}$ and $(\beta_k)_{k \in (1,...,q)}$ are bounded integral operators in $L^2[0,1]^2$ given by $(\alpha_jx)(t)=\int \alpha_j(t,s)x(s)ds$ and $(\beta_kx)(t)=\int \beta_k(t,s)x(s)ds$ that map non-negative functions to non-negative functions for $x\in L^2[0,1]$. By convention, I restrict the intraday time variable to $t\in[0,1]$. This is without loss of generality since data that do not follow this convention can be converted into this format. Accordingly, $\int_0^1$ will be abbreviated by $\int$. The second time variable $i$ denotes the trading day. The innovations $(\varepsilon_i(t))_{i\in\mathbb{Z}}$ are i.i.d. random functions.\\
From \Cref{fGARCHpq}, it is clear that the FGARCH$(p,q)$ process is the functional analogue of the strong (scalar) GARCH process. As a consequence, the model admits an ARMA-type representation, just as in the scalar case discussed in \Cref{ARMAstructure}.\\
Following \citet{Aue2017} and \citet{RICE20201023}, I focus on the FGARCH$(1,1)$ specification, which is the functional counterpart of the benchmark strong (scalar) GARCH$(1,1)$ model. As discussed previously, despite its simplicity the strong GARCH(1,1) process is also one of the most widely used specifications in practice. For this reason, the FGARCH$(1,1)$ model is a natural starting point in the functional setting. In this case, the FGARCH$(p,q)$ equations reduce to
\begin{gather}
    \label{FunctionalGARCH11}
    y_i(t) = \sigma_i(t)\varepsilon_i(t)\\
    \label{FunctionalGARCH11volatility}
    \sigma_i^2(t) = \delta(t)+\alpha y_{i-1}^2(t)+\beta\sigma_{i-1}^2(t).
\end{gather}
In the following, conditions for the existence of a stationary solution to the FGARCH$(1,1)$ process are discussed. \citet{Aue2017} derives these results both for the space $L^2[0,1]$ as well as for the space of continuous functions $C[0,1]$. I restrict attention to the $L^2[0,1]$ setting, since the corresponding results for $C[0,1]$ are of essentially the same form. The main difference is that the innovation functions and the intercept function $\delta(t)$ are assumed to belong to $C[0,1]$, whereas the kernel of the integral operators remain in $L^2[0,1]^2$, so that the resulting operators map $C[0,1]$ into $C[0,1]$.\\
Using notation analogous to the scalar case, the volatility equation can be written as
\begin{gather}
\label{FGARCH11volatilityalternative}
    \sigma_i^2(t) = \delta(t) + \gamma_{i-1} \sigma_{i-1}^2(t), \quad \text{where }\gamma_i(t,s)=\alpha(t,s)\varepsilon_i^2(s)+\beta(t,s).
\end{gather}
The following theorem provides sufficient conditions for the existence of a strictly stationary solution.
\begin{theorem}[Strict stationarity of the FGARCH(1,1) process]
\label{StationarityFGARCH11}
    Let $\delta(t)\in L^2[0,1]$ be a non-negative function, and let $\alpha$ and $\beta$ be integral operators with kernels in $L^2[0,1]^2$ that map non-negative functions to non-negative functions. Let $\{\varepsilon_i(t):i\in \mathbb{Z}\}$ be i.i.d. random functions and $\varepsilon_0\in L^{4}[0,1]$ almost surely.
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
        \item If
            \begin{gather}
            \label{FGARCH11StationaryCondition}
                \infty\leq \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{log\left\| \gamma_0 \right\|_\mathcal{S}}]<0
            \end{gather}
            then the infinite sum
            \begin{gather}
            \label{FGARCH11StationarySolution}
                h_i(t)=\delta(t)+\sum_{k=1}^\infty \Gamma_{i,k}\delta(t)
            \end{gather}
            converges almost surely, and the process $(y_i(t))$ defined by $y_i(t)=\sqrt{h_i(t)}\varepsilon_i(t)$ is the unique strictly stationary solution of the FGARCH(1,1) model in \labelcref{FunctionalGARCH11} and \labelcref{FunctionalGARCH11volatility}. This solution is non-anticipative and ergodic.
        \item If there is $\nu>0$ such that 
        \begin{gather}
        \label{FGARCH11StationarityMomentsCondition}
            \mathbb{E}[\left\| \gamma_0 \right\|_\mathcal{S}^\nu ]<1
        \end{gather}
        then there exists a unique, strictly stationary, and nonanticipative solution in $L^2[0,1]$ to the FGARCH(1,1) model and $\mathbb{E}[\left\| \sigma_0^2 \right\|_2^\nu]<\infty$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
Here $\left\|\right\|_\mathcal{S}$ is the Hilbert-Schmidt norm defined as
\begin{gather}
    \left\| \gamma_0 \right\|_\mathcal{S} = \left( \int\int \gamma^2_0(t,s)dt ds \right)^{\frac{1}{2}}
\end{gather}
and $\Gamma_{i,k}$ is the following random integral operator
\begin{gather}
    (\Gamma_{i,k}x)(t)=\int ...\int \gamma_{i-1}(t,s_1)\gamma_{i-2}(s_1,s_2)...\gamma_{i-k}(s_{k-1},s_k)x(s_k)ds_k...ds_2ds_1.
\end{gather}
Due to the close resemblance between this theorem and \Cref{stationarityscalarGARCH11}, the proof of \Cref{StationarityFGARCH11} follows analogous arguments. First, $h_i(t)$ is obtained by recursively solving \labelcref{FGARCH11volatilityalternative}. Second, the convergence of $h_i(t)$ is established by applying the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality in conjunction with the strong law of large numbers. Finally, the uniqueness of the solution is shown by exploiting the recursive structure of \labelcref{FGARCH11volatilityalternative}. Assuming the existence of an alternative solution implies that both solutions converge to the same limit, which in turn forces them to coincide, thereby yielding a contradiction. The full proof of \Cref{StationarityFGARCH11} can be found in the Appendix.\\

In this section, the estimation of an FGARCH(1,1) model will be discussed. I consider a setting in which a finite sample of $N$ functional observations $\{y_i(t)\}_{i=1}^N$ is available. Although functional data are commonly recorded on a dense discrete grid in practical applications, I assume here that the underlying curves are directly observed or can be reliably reconstructed from the data.\\
Because the objects to be estimated are infinite-dimensional, direct estimation from a finite sample is generally ill-posed and therefore requires some form of dimension reduction. For this reason, \citet{Aue2017} impose a standard finite-dimensional approximation on the intercept function $\delta(t)$ and on the integral operators $\alpha$ and $\beta$. In particular, they assume that all three objects can be approximated using a fixed number $M$ of orthonormal basis functions $\Phi_M = \{\varphi_1(t), \ldots, \varphi_M(t)\}$ defined on $[0,1]$. Under this assumption, $\delta(t)$ is represented as a finite linear combination of these basis functions, while the operators $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are represented through their kernel functions using the same basis:
\begin{align}
\label{basisdecompositiondelta}
    \delta(t) &= \sum_{m=1}^M d_m \varphi_m(t), \\
    \alpha(t,s) &= \sum_{m,m'=1}^M a_{m,m'} \varphi_m(t)\varphi_{m'}(s), \\
    \label{basisdecompositionbeta}
    \beta(t,s) &= \sum_{m,m'=1}^M b_{m,m'} \varphi_m(t)\varphi_{m'}(s).
\end{align}
From this representation, it is easy to see that estimating the functional objects $\delta(t)$, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ simplifies to estimating $\{d_1,\ldots,d_M,a_{1,1},\ldots,a_{M,M},b_{1,1},\ldots,b_{M,M}\}$. To further simplify notation, \citet{Aue2017} define the following quantities:
\begin{align}
\label{MatricesdAB}
    \mathbf{d}=\begin{pmatrix}
        d_1\\
        \vdots\\
        d_M
    \end{pmatrix}; \quad
    \mathbf{A}=\begin{pmatrix}
        a_{1,1} & \ldots & a_{1,M}\\
        \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
        a_{M,1} & \ldots & a_{M,M}
    \end{pmatrix}; \quad
    \mathbf{B}=\begin{pmatrix}
        b_{1,1} & \ldots & b_{1,M}\\
        \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
        b_{M,1} & \ldots & b_{M,M}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{align}
To estimate $\mathbf{d}$, $\mathbf{A}$, and $\mathbf{B}$, the squared observations $(y_i^2(t))_{i\in[1,N]}$ and conditional volatility functions $(\sigma_i^2(t))_{i\in[1,N]}$ are projected onto $\Phi_M$. This yields the M-dimensional vectors $\mathbf{y}_i^{(2)}=\begin{pmatrix}
    y_{i,1}^{(2)} & \ldots & y_{i,M}^{(2)}
\end{pmatrix}^\top$ and $\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}=\begin{pmatrix}
    s_{i,1}^{(2)} & \ldots & s_{i,M}^{(2)}
\end{pmatrix}^\top$ whose components are given by the inner products with the basis functions in $\Phi_M$ $y_{i,m}^{(2)}=\langle y_i^2(t),\varphi_m(t)\rangle$, $s_{i,m}^{(2)}=\langle \sigma_i^2(t),\varphi_m(t)\rangle$. Under the finite-dimensional parametrisation introduced above, the volatility recursion can then be written in terms of the projected coefficient vectors as
\begin{align}
\label{FGARCH11MatrixForm}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}=\mathbf{d}+\mathbf{Ay}_{i-1}^{(2)}+\mathbf{Bs}_{i-1}^{(2)}.
\end{align}
Solving this equation recursively obtains an infinite-order representation for $\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}$ that only depends on $\mathbf{y}_{i}^{(2)}$
\begin{align}
\label{Matrixrepinftrealparameters}
    \mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}=\sum_{\ell=1}^{\infty} \mathbf{B}^{\ell-1}\mathbf{d}+\sum_{\ell=1}^{\infty}\mathbf{B}^{\ell-1}\mathbf{Ay}_{i-\ell}^{(2)}.
\end{align}
Since the true parameter vector $\mathbf{\theta}=(\mathbf{d}^{\top},\mathbf{A}^\top,\mathbf{B}^\top)^\top$ is unknown, \labelcref{Matrixrepinftrealparameters} is expressed at an arbitrary parameter value $\mathbf{\underline{\theta}}=(\underline{\mathbf{d}}^{\top},\underline{\mathbf{A}}^\top,\underline{\mathbf{B}}^\top)^\top\in\mathbf{\Theta}\subset\mathbb{R}^{M+2M^2}$
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{\tilde{s}}_i^{(2)}=\mathbf{\tilde{s}}_i^{(2)}(\underline{\theta})=\sum_{\ell=1}^{\infty}\underline{\mathbf{B}}^{\ell-1}\underline{\mathbf{d}}+\sum_{\ell=1}^{\infty}\underline{\mathbf{B}}^{\ell-1}\underline{\mathbf{A}}\mathbf{y}_{i-\ell}^{(2)}.
\end{align}
Since in practice only a finite time frame of the series can be observed, $\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}$ is approximated by $\mathbf{\hat{s}}_i^{(2)}$
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{\hat{s}}_i^{(2)}=\mathbf{\hat{s}}_i^{(2)}(\underline{\theta})=\sum_{\ell=1}^{i-1}\underline{\mathbf{B}}^{\ell-1}\underline{\mathbf{d}}+\sum_{\ell=1}^{i-1}\underline{\mathbf{B}}^{\ell-1}\underline{\mathbf{A}}\mathbf{y}_{i-\ell}^{(2)}.
\end{align}
The objective, thus, has to be to choose an estimator $\mathbf{\underline{\hat{\theta}}}_n$ such that $\mathbf{\hat{s}}_i^{(2)}$ is close to $\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}$. Since $\mathbf{s}_i^{(2)}$ is unobservable, \citet{Aue2017} impose
\begin{gather}
\label{UnitVarianceepsilonfunction}
    \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_i^2(t)]=1; \quad \forall t\in[0,1].
\end{gather}
This is without loss of generality since this condition can always be fulfilled by an appropriate way of scaling. Under this condition, $\mathbb{E}[y_i^{2}(t)|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}]=\sigma_i^{2(t)}$ and therefore, after projection onto $\Phi_M$, $\mathbb{E}[y_i^{(2)}|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}]=s_i^{(2)}$ where $\mathcal{F}_{i-1}$ is the sigma-algebra generated by all $\varepsilon_\ell:\ell<i$, $\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=\sigma(\varepsilon_\ell:\ell<i)$. As a consequence, \citet{Aue2017} define the following least squares estimator
\begin{align}
\label{ObjectiveFunctionLSFGARCH}
    \underline{\hat{\theta}}_n=\arg \min\{S_n(\underline{\theta})=\sum_{i=2}^n\{y_i^{(2)}-\hat{s}_i^{(2)}(\underline{\theta}) \}^{\top}\{y_i^{(2)}-\hat{s}_i^{(2)}(\underline{\theta}) \}:\underline{\theta}\in\mathbf{\Theta}\}.
\end{align}

\end{gather}

\subsection{Value-at-risk}
\label{subsec:ValueAtRisk}
%\begin{itemize}
%    \item Explanation of the concept of value-at-risk
%\end{itemize}
In this section, I will give a quick overview of the concept of value-at-risk. I will focus on the definition of value-at-risk as well as the criticisms that the concept of value-at-risk has faced.\\
Value-at-risk is intended to specify a limit below which the value of, for example, a security cannot fall with a predetermined probability. In probability terms, this means that under the assumption that the map $x\rightarrow \mathbb{P}(y_i\leq x|\mathcal{F}_{i-1})$ is strictly increasing where $\mathcal{F}_{i-1}$ denotes the $\sigma$-algebra up to $i-1$
\begin{gather}
    \mathbb{P}(y_i\leq \text{VaR}_i^\tau|\mathcal{F}_{i-1})=\tau.
\end{gather}
$\text{VaR}_i^\tau$ here denotes the almost surely unique $\mathcal{F}_{i-1}$-measurable random variable at quantile $\tau$.\\
From this definition, two important properties of value-at-risk can be inferred. First, value-at-risk is not subadditive, meaning that the sum of the individual value-at-risk of two portfolios is not bigger or equal to the value-of-risk of a portfolio combining the two portfolios. As a consequence, value-at-risk is not a coherent risk measure \citep{Artzner1999}. To see this, think of the following example: Let there be two random variables A and B that are independent of one another. Both A and B have the same distribution function. For both A and B $\mathbb{P}(A|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=0)=0.95$ ($\mathbb{P}(B|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=0)=0.95$) and $\mathbb{P}(A|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=-10)=0.05$ ($\mathbb{P}(B|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=-10)=0.05$). The value-at-risk for each individual random variable at a confidence level of $\tau=0.05$ is equal to $0$. However, the combined value-at-risk for the two random variables is $-10$. This is because the combined probability $\mathbb{P}(A|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=0 \land B|\mathcal{F}_{i-1}=0)=0.95 \cdot 0.95 = 0.9025$.\\
A second property of value-at-risk is that it is insensitive to the underlying distribution of the random variable, and in particular does not account for the behavior of the distribution’s tail.\\
These two features combined with the usage of value-at-risk as a risk management tool have led to the concept of value-at-risk being heavily criticized. As a result, extensions to value-at-risk have been established. The most important extension to value-at-risk is Expected Shortfall or Conditional value-at-risk. Expected shortfall is defined as follows
\begin{gather}
    ES_i^\tau(t)= \mathbb{E}[y_i(t)|\mathcal{F}_{i-1},y_i(t)<VaR_i^\tau(t)].
\end{gather}
Expected Shortfall is subadditive and takes the tails of the distribution into consideration. Therefore, Expected Shortfalls addresses the two main theoretical issues of value-at-risk. \citet{RICE20201023} also considers expected shortfall in the appendix of their paper. They show that the method explained in \Cref{sec:Simulation} can easily be extended to the concept of Expected Shortfall.

In the next section, I will explore the finite sample properties of the FGARCH model and highlight a few practical considerations that have to be made when using the FGARCH model for forecasting intra-day value-at-risk in practice.\\
First, it should be emphasized that functional data are, in practice, observed on a finite grid. Consequently, both the properties discussed in \Cref{subsec:FGARCHModel} and the estimation procedure described in \Cref{subsec:FGARCHEstimation} are implemented in a discretized framework. This discretization does not yield a purely multivariate GARCH model; rather, it leads to a finite-dimensional approximation of the underlying functional model. In particular, operations defined in the functional setting—such as integrals in inner products—must be approximated numerically, which requires explicit consideration of the grid structure and the distances between adjacent grid points. For all simulations, functional data are approximated on an equidistant grid between $[0,1]$ with 200 grid points.\\
Second, to forecast value-at-risk, an appropriate method has to be used to approximate the pointwise distribution of the innovation process $\varepsilon_i(t)$. Then the value-at-risk forecast is equal to $\hat{\sigma}_i(t)\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ where $\hat{\sigma}_i(t)$ is the forecasted value of $\sigma_i(t)$ using the fitted FGARCH model and $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ is the estimated $\tau$ quantile of $\varepsilon_i(t)$. To estimate $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ \citet{RICE20201023} propose three methods. For the first method, \citeauthor{RICE20201023} impose that $\varepsilon_i(t)$ follows a Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. The second method estimates $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ using a bootstrap algorithm. Method three utilizes extreme value theory to better model the tails of the distribution. I will focus on the second method since it performed the best in practice \citep{RICE20201023}.\\
To obtain $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ using the bootstrap algorithm first the fitted $\hat{\sigma}_i(t)$ needs to be computed. Next, $\hat{\varepsilon}_i(t)$ is calculated by $\hat{\varepsilon}_i(t)=\frac{y_i(t)}{\hat{\sigma}_i(t)}$. Finally, to get $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ the estimated error terms are resampled $B$ many times with replacement. $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^\tau(t)$ is then equal to the pointwise $\tau$-quantile of $(\hat{\varepsilon}_i^{(b)}(t))_{b\in[1,B]}$.\\

This definition is taken from \citet{francq2019garch}.
\begin{definition}[Ergodic stationary process]
    A real valued strictly stationary process $(Z_i)_{i\in\mathbb{Z}}$ is said to be ergodic if and only if, for any Borel set $B$ and any integer $k$,
    \begin{gather}
        \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n  \mathbb{1}_B(Z_i,Z_{i+1},...,Z_{i+k})\rightarrow \mathbb{P}[(Z_1,...,Z_{1+k})\in B]
    \end{gather}
    with probability 1.
\end{definition}
It should be noted that the concept of ergodicity is much more general and can be extended to non-stationary sequences (see, for example, \citet{billingsley1995probability}).\\
The ergodic theorem applied to stationary sequences states the following.
\begin{theorem}[The ergodic theorem for stationary sequences]
    If $(Z_i)_{i\in\mathbb{Z}}$ is strictly stationary and ergodic, if $f$ is measurable and if 
    \begin{gather}
        \mathbb{E}|f(...,Z_{i-1},Z_i,Z_{i+1},...)|<\infty
    \end{gather}
    then
    \begin{gather}
        \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n f(...,Z_{i-1},Z_i,Z_{i+1},...)\rightarrow\mathbb{E}[f(...,Z_{i-1},Z_i,Z_{i+1},...)]\quad a.s.
    \end{gather}
\end{theorem}
